## What to Skip

- **Blenders advertising "800W" with no corroborating reviews.** Wattage claims on no-name budget blenders are frequently exaggerated. Stick to brands with a verifiable track record.
- **Fixed (non-detachable) blending heads.** If you can't remove the blending shaft from the motor body, cleaning is miserable and the motor seal is more likely to fail over time from moisture exposure.
- **Single-speed stick blenders for anything beyond basic tasks.** One speed is fine for a simple smoothie. If you ever want to make mayo, whipped cream, or a chunky salsa, you need at least a low/high option — ideally variable control.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can an immersion blender under $50 really handle hot soup?**
Yes — most corded models in this price range handle hot liquids just fine. The key is to keep the blade fully submerged while blending (tilt the pot slightly if needed) and use a deep, narrow container to minimize splatter. The Breville's anti-suction design makes this especially foolproof.

**What wattage do I need in an immersion blender?**
For everyday tasks like soups and smoothies, 200–400 watts is sufficient. If you want to blend fibrous greens, frozen fruit, or nut-based sauces, aim for 500 watts or higher. The Turelar's 1100 watts is exceptional for this price tier.

**Are cordless immersion blenders worth it under $50?**
At this price point, not really. Cordless models in the sub-$50 range typically sacrifice meaningful battery life and motor power for the convenience factor. If cord-free matters to you, it's worth stretching your budget. For most home cooks, a corded model is the smarter buy here.
