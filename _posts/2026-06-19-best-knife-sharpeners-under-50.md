## What to Skip

- **Single-stage sharpeners under $10.** That little V-shaped plastic tool rattling around your junk drawer isn't sharpening your knife — it's scratching the edge and calling it done. You need at least two stages to get a usable result.
- **Electric sharpeners under $30.** At this price, the motors are underpowered and the angle guides are imprecise. You'll either remove too much metal or not enough. Save electric for a higher budget.
- **Any sharpener with no non-slip base.** If it can slide while you're using it, it's a hazard. Check that whatever you buy has rubber feet or a grip base before you put a knife through it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I use a knife sharpener?**
For a home kitchen getting regular use, running your knives through a fine or honing stage every two to four weeks is plenty. Save the coarse or diamond stages for when the edge is genuinely damaged — overusing heavy abrasives shortens your knife's lifespan unnecessarily.

**What's the difference between honing and sharpening?**
Sharpening removes metal to create a new edge. Honing realigns an existing edge that's bent or rolled from use. Most multi-stage pull-through sharpeners do both — earlier slots sharpen, later ceramic slots hone. For regular maintenance between sharpenings, the honing slot is your go-to.

**Are pull-through sharpeners safe for expensive knives?**
They're fine for mid-range and everyday kitchen knives. If you're working with Japanese knives or high-carbon blades with very specific bevel angles, a whetstone gives you more control. For the average home cook's German-steel chef's knife, a quality pull-through sharpener under $50 will serve you well for years.
